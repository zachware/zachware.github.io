<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Some time ago I started treating my health as a job, on par with my company and my relationships. This meant it required more analysis and study.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>How can I naturally intervene in my own biology to bring my systems as close to homeostasis as possible?</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Biohacking is a loaded term. Most of what you read about it seems to profile attempts to use unnatural interventions (pharma, weird tools, crazy sleep cycles) in search of immediate measureable outcome like massive productivity or a long-term immeasurable benefit like living to 100. And they generally violate my basic rule that the things I do have to be compatible with living a normal life where your friends and partner don't find you insufferable to be around.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Further, many of these interventions are like lurching for a piece of gold in front of a dump truck. Many of the tools and pharmacological interventions have minimal data on efficacy and risk over long periods of time. I'm going for a piece of gold in front of a tricycle. High possible ROI with minimal unknown risk.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:heading -->
<h2><strong>Inversion</strong></h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>I think of healthspan and lifespan through the Munger lens of inversion. Munger says "All&nbsp;I want&nbsp;to&nbsp;know&nbsp;is where&nbsp;I'm going to die,&nbsp;so&nbsp;I'll&nbsp;never go there." Thankfully human biology gives us strong empirical clues about <em>where</em> <a href="http://80% of deaths in people over 50 who do not smoke">the 80% of us who don't smoke are going to die</a>. The problem is that preventing these killers starts decades before they come after us. </p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>The human body is a magical organism when it's operating at homeostasis. Modern society is in a constant battle with that equilibrium so my focus is on avoiding those negative inputs, increasing the positive inputs, measuring the outcomes and iterating. </p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Every month I do a deep personal review of my "performance" and data supplemented with quarterly blood panels with my functional medicine doctor. I shared one of these reviews with a few friends recently and they encouraged me to share my "health stack."</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>This post is an attempt to do that in summary form with a lot of attention paid to easy starting points versus being a 20-step guide to living forever. When I share my lifestyle choices with close friends I'm careful to explain three things.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list {"ordered":true} -->
<ol><li>This is my hobby. Some people spend hours per week playing golf. This is my golf. I don't advocate my craziness as a path for people who aren't as interested in it as I am.</li><li>I read primary source data and spend time learning from people operating in the realm of data who are curious and eager to be disproven (e.g. no Dr. Oz).</li><li>Most importantly, everything I do has an MVP version. For example, getting better sleep doesn't involve a 45 step process and  buying $5000 of beds, a sauna, and sleep gear. There are small steps you can take to make huge improvements based on what you learn from the more complicated experiments. 1% better every day, that's the mantra. </li></ol>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Finally, I am not a medical doctor. While much of this post is based on the work of MDs and scientists in various fields including lipidology, neuroscience, oncology, etc. <em>this is not medical advice</em>. Do as I've done and find a smart functional medicine MD before acting on anything. </p>
