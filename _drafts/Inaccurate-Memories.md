When I was in high school I wrote a long-form narrative about the death of my father, who passed away when I was six. It won wide praise. It was published in the school's literary magazine. I seem to recall it was named among the best pieces of the year.

My mother, who was obviously much older than I at the time my father died, read the story and told me that none of the details I remember about that day were accurate. The details of that day are so incredibly fresh, even today. But I've since spoken to a few other people and they've confirmed my mother's feedback. My brain's construct of that day is complete fantasy.

How could my memory of that day be so inaccurate? And what does that tell me about myself?

This was one of the most important lessons of my life. And it's influenced me in profound ways since. I give this experience credit for creating the near obsessive level of self-examination that's defined my adult life.

<a href="https://www.farnamstreetblog.com/2017/04/letting-go-self-justification/?utm_source=feedburner&amp;utm_medium=twitter&amp;utm_campaign=Feed%3A+68131+%28Farnam+Street%29">Farnam Street has an amazing piece this morning</a> about our tendencies to remember the past in ways that support our current self-narrative rather than accurately.

<blockquote>We remember our past <a href="https://www.farnamstreetblog.com/2016/08/many-ways-memory-fails-us-part-3/">in a way that confirms what we believe of ourselves in the present</a>.</blockquote>

The inconvenience is well explained:

<blockquote><strong>When we use our memories to strengthen our narratives, it causes huge dissonance when those memories are revealed to be false. It often requires a rewriting of the entire narrative.</strong></blockquote>

From the book <a href="https://www.amazon.com/Mistakes-Were-Made-But-Not/dp/1491514132/ref=as_li_ss_tl?sa-no-redirect=1&amp;linkCode=ll1&amp;tag=zgwareblog-20&amp;linkId=eb6416ad82382e1c4b9185a47b5b1695">Mistakes Were Made (But Not by Me</a>), Carol Tavris and Elliot Aronson, FS cites a passage:

<blockquote>If a memory is a central part of your identity, a self-serving distortion is even more likely.” How many of us have come across our old journals or diaries, and felt like they were reading the life story of someone else? We will frequently look at the entries and say ‘wow, I don’t remember being like this at all’.</blockquote>

FS brings this back to today. Memories are subject to editorial influence. We remember the details that help us maintain our self-image:

<blockquote>In order to be able to admit mistakes, to correct the spiraling self-justification that can have devastating consequences for ourselves and others, we need to accept that “something we did can be separated from who we are, and who we want to be. Our past selves need not be a blueprint for our future selves. The road to redemption starts with the understanding that who we are includes what we have done but also transcends it, and the vehicle for transcending it is self-compassion.”</blockquote>

In summary:

<blockquote>The [book] authors argue that is a problem, particularly endemic to North America, that we associate mistakes with failure. We need to shift the thinking, to see mistakes as part of the learning process, necessary steps on the <a href="https://www.farnamstreetblog.com/2014/09/mistakes">road to making things better</a>.</blockquote>

For the nerds, take a look at <a href="http://cogprints.org/600/1/199802010.html">this highly-cited 1993 scientific paper</a> about the explosion of repressed traumatic memories that came about in the 90s.
