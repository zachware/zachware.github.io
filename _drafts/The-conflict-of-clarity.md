<em>Take the time to decide what you think. -??</em>

I value clear thinking more than I value most other aspects of work and, to a degree, life.

I spend large blocks of time thinking. To the outsider this may appear to be a path of leisure. I must, after all, just be sitting around doddling.

In reality I'm agonizing. The painful mental cycles I force myself through to go from a disjointed, wide set of views on topics in my life to a clear, simple view are absurd.

My process is to learn. I read books. I read articles. I reach out to experts. In the words of Warren Buffett I "do the work required to have an opinion."

Only when I've done that do I feel that I've earned the right to have a conversation about a topic. I come to that conversation with a view and use what I've learned to support the view I carry. I want nothing more than to be convinced that I'm wrong. This, after all, is how we learn and improve.

In my work as an investor this involves studying industries or companies. It involves attempting to find patterns and lateral connections between unrelated topics.

I discover a probem, I do the work required to have an opinion, then I work through the problem with my team. We parse, we decide, we proceed. It's a loop. And when we have new data, we repeat the process.

For years my approach was subtly different. I did the work, but I was afraid to develop my own opinion. I felt that if I dug deep enough, I could uncover a clear view from someone I and others respected and, in a sense, proxy that view as my own. I valued open, unstructured discussion more than deep learning. So my argument became "she says x" or "data says y." And I would leave it at that. Or I would fall into the trap most organizations fall into. I would just keep talking about it ad nauseum.

I realized I was taking the easy path. I wasn't using problems as an opportunity to learn so much as I was using them as an opportunity to fill time. Meetings and conversations looped on end. And in the cases where I or someone else channeled someone else's view to develop our own, I was prioritizing conflict-avoidance over getting better.

This was a superpower. For example, in an organization if I said "the CEO things x" then that had the function of deflecting ownership to someone not in the room. No one took a risk. No one got smarter.

As I've grown older and (hopefully) wiser I've realized the fallacy of this approach. I hid behind the opinions of superheroes. I confused talking with doing. I allowed conversations to be on the surface.

I deliberately took a left turn. Today I take the time to learn. When I develop a view it's clear. And I can back it up. And I do so directly. If I don't have a view or if I don't feel I have the right to one, I try to stand back and listen or simply take note and later dive deeper into my agonizing process. Then I come back with a view.

There is a down side to this approach. Working with me can be hard. My style is direct and borderline aggressive. It can seem as though I am arguing <em>for</em> most things that I'm actually indifferent about. I'm just hoping to use a view as a basis for a conversation that helps that view grow clearer.

My approach can be offputting for some more used to environments that place a higher value on interpersonal meshing than on encouraging direct arguments. I am comfortable valuing the opposite.

Situations and sometimes relationships go south, however, when everyone's expectations are not clear. Imagine coming to a tennis match only to discover that your opponent was expecting to play with a baseball bat and a golf ball. Everyone leaves feeling bad not so much about what happened but because of what happened relative to what they expected.

So as happy as I am with my approach, I want to spend more time with people developing clear expectations.

My directness is most intolerable when a conflict centers not as much around comparing one view to the other, but where the participants, perhaps including myself, have not taken the time to develop a view of their own.

To illustrate imagine two people playing a tennis match. To play tennis one needs a racquet, a ball, a court and an agreement on the rules of engagement. I play hard.
